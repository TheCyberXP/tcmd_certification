Critical Questions

Can you answer the following:


What problem(s) am I solving?


Who are my key users?


What are their needs?

When developing any application you need to as these fundamental questions about the consumer.
What problems are you solving,
Who are your key users,
What are their needs
These questions form the foundation of what you build your core application around.

Focus on Needs

Segment Your Customers


Focus on your primary consumer! (The one that makes you profitable)


Remove your personal opinion! (You're not the target consumer)

Let's focus on your customer for a minute. This is important for everyone that touches the project to be aware of so that it drives the right experience for the user.
- Focus on your primary consumer. Which one makes you the money? If there are more than 5 segments to your market, pick the top two. You will not be able to please everyone with your app. That is why there is more than 90,000 of them in the app store. This is why we see massive updates to how apps work.
- Example: A good example of this is the Facebook app. If you have Facebook apps or pages, you can't use their app to control them. This is by design, as 600 million people want to read their news feed, and only a fraction of a percentile of those people will manage their pages.
- Remove your personal opinion. If you are working on the project you are probably to close to the product. Always revert back to the consumer and ask “Is it what they need”.
- Recommendation: Put a picture of your key consumer on your desk when working on the project to remind you who this is for.

Hardware

Hardware is confining by nature


Freedom in the framework


Use it to your advantage


Work with the lowest common greatest value


Provide added values

  • Hardware constraints: screen size, input options, memory limitations
  • Freedom in framework = understanding what is available to you and how to truly utilize it to your advantage.
  • Advantage = new ways to engage customers, video, audio, location, etc. Those things that mobile does well, but desktop doesn't
  • Target lowest common hardware that provides the greatest value to your company. Drop support for older devices or OSes when they're not big segments of your userbase
  • Provide added value = giving users reason to use your app

Device Principles

You are married to your mobile


Interfaces are truncated


Design for real mobile interfaces (voice, messaging, location...)


'Plan for Abandon'

Married to mobile — take it with you everywhere (bedroom, bathroom, work, play)
Truncated — small, limited input options, one handed operation
Mobile — hub for messaging, voice, location, network
Plan for Abandon = interruptions are constant — phone calls, people interrupting, etc. Average app use time is a few seconds. Plan your UX around that.

Screens

Do NOT design once, place everywhere


Resolutions are opportunity


Target your majority

Take advantage of the differences in screens and sizes
Optimize for the difference in screen real estate and resolution
Target the hardware of the majority of users

Accelerometer

Directional Information


Use in conjunction with Compass


Directional Scrolling


Dictates Orientation

Accelerometer tells you how the device is moving. In conjunction with the compass, you can tell where and how the user is looking

Novel inputs with the accelerometer:
  • Tip to move to next screen/page
  • Roll a ball or move a 'player' in a game
  • 'Throw' to transfer data

Orientation

You must lock your orientation, or design for both!


User's rotate device for a variety or reasons, your app must work in both orientations


Better yet, design for both!


We'll talk in another lesson how to handle. This is more the why

Apple's HIG says iPad should account for all four orientations
iPhone should be either:
  • locked to portrait
  • or, portrait, landscape left/right but not upside down so that folks don't accidentally have their phone upside down when making/receiving a call

Orientation

'Jamie’s Recipes' — not a Titanium app

Navigation
Navigation is really the core behind your UX
Must be simple and intuitive
Test with real users

Let's take a look at the common elements and explore which work best as a base before expanding on how to build with them.

Tabs

Decision Powered

Quick & Efficient

Natural User Experience

Instant Information Use

Tabs imply equal weight, not a hierarchy
Related functionality under the common purpose of the app
Tabs are very familiar to users
They are quick and efficient to use
Tab based apps are the most popular apps that exist. The reason it that there is no more than five or so decisions that a user needs to make.

Modal Windows

Signify separate action


Path must lead back to purpose


Easy creation & close


Expected "inconvenience"

login screens, alerts, etc. — stop user action
They are an expected UX component
But make them useful and part of the experience
There must be a clear path (app flow) provided by the modal windows
Programming wise, these windows are easy to create and close

Window Navigation

Plan for simple, intuitive navigation

Use storyboarding tools, like those in Xcode

But remember your Minimum Viable Product — just what's needed, no feature creep

Topic

Indication & Staus
Loading indicators, alerts & messages, etc.
Indicators and status are often overlooked
Must provide the necessary feedback so a user knows what is going on.
When actions fail or time-out, users need to know

Loading Indicators

Provide feedback to user


Provide information


Show progress


Pass/Fail


Did it work? Is it done?
Loading indicators provide critical feedback to a user.
Without notification, users can misunderstand whether an action worked, or failed, or is complete, etc.
Think uploads & downloads, logging in and out, progress through a series of data entry steps

Feedback

Alerts are ok


Action screens are good


Active results are best


  • Alerts are in your face, but get the job done
  • Action screens — customized screen, fills entire viewport, opportunity for better UI
  • Active results — best, inline with rest of app's actions — game results, incoming messages, users get info and can react

Feedback should give positive reinforcement of desired actions in your app, and discourage actions that aren't the best way to use your app.

With that being said... (go to next slide)

Don't Over Dialog

Only provide it when necessary


Don't remind me constantly


Avoid repetitive loops

Constant nagging will drive away users
Provide just enough, but not too much feedback
Not every action needs some sort of 'Congratulations' message
Avoid loops and situations that cause extensive dialogs. I shouldn't have to be informed more than once to do something, especially if I'm wrong.

Don't Over Inform

Are you serving the needs?


Does the data really fit?


Are you just showing off?

Just because you have data doesn't mean it needs to be shown.

You have to go back to the core three questions:
What problem(s) am I solving?
Who are my key users?
What are their needs?
In light of the answers to these questions, ask yourself if the data really fits and needs to be shown now. If you cannot justify why the data is there, then it probably doesn't't belong there.

Development & Testing

How to Test

6 people is enough, no really it is!

Paper mockups work

LiveView apps work well

Do not 'own' the project

In our opinion, Small groups are best, 6 is enough — more than that and you get too many divergent opinions and you're probably not targeting your primary audience with that larger group.

Ask specific questions rather than 'what do you think?'

Paper apps work fine for testing

Pass off project as someone else's to remove personal concerns

Using Titanium for Rapid Testing

Bring your UI to life

Bring important Design elements (Fonts, colors, positioning)

Place dead images

Insert 'Lorem Ipsum'

You can use mock screens / static graphics to further test your UX
Create static images, lay transparent clickable views atop the images to provide rudimentary navigation and flow to bring a UI mockup to live.
Use dummy images as placeholders, especially for user-submitted images.
Do NOT use real copy, use Lorem Ipsum text, otherwise testers will focus on misspellings and grammar.

Iteration

Change early and often

Ensure your changes are justified

Pivoting 180 degrees is not bad

Ask yourself the three questions...

Change early/often means to iterate, try/change/try/change
Always go back to your core three questions:
What problems are you solving,
Who are your key users,
What are their needs

Continual Testing

Keep testing hypotheses

You are never complete!

Remember your MVP

Many of our app developers build in analytics so they can test UX even after they publish their apps. Features that aren't used, actions that are performed incorrectly, etc. are logged and improved or removed in the next release.
Remember your minimum viable product
If nothing else, make sure you provide true value to your user, and that you are satisfying their needs.
